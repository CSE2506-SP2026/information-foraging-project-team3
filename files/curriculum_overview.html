Curriculum Overview

Washington University Nursery School uses a child-centered, play-based curriculum that supports the social, emotional, intellectual, and physical development of each child. The sections below highlights the core components of our curriculum, which are designed to foster a love of learning and promote the growth of the whole child.

Curriculum Philosophy

At Washington University Nursery School, we believe children are unique individuals with their own patterns and timetables for development. We support and encourage growth through a child-centered, process-oriented, play-based curriculum.

Teachers build learning experiences by:

  • Structuring experiences around ideas relevant to children, with learning happening through discussion, experimentation, and meaningful materials
  • Offering hands-on activities that engage multiple senses and encourage exploration
  • Letting children interact at their own pace (individually, with partners, or in groups)
  • Asking open-ended questions and giving children time to think, respond, and reason
  • Respecting children’s ideas and building independence and problem-solving skills
  • Using a diverse, multi-cultural community to celebrate similarities and differences across families and cultures

How We Provide Extra Help

Teachers observe and assess children's progress on an ongoing basis and adapt experiences within the curriculum to meet the needs of each child.

If one or more children need help in a specific area, teachers may adapt the curriculum by:

  • Offering more one-on-one teacher-student time to understand the child’s thinking process, build trust, and increase confidence
  • Offering more opportunities for exploration
  • Simplifying an activity by removing stimuli
  • Expanding an activity by adding components for exploration
  • Appealing to different senses and ways of exploring materials to engage learning in new ways
  • Planning individual, small group, and/or large group activities around a particular concept, skill, or area of concern

Social & Emotional Development

Individual children's capabilities are valued and strengthened within the group throughout the curriculum by:

  • Building self-esteem through communication, planning, responsibility, problem-solving, participation, following directions, imagination, and sharing
  • Developing collaboration, compromise, self-expression, and respect for self and others through experience, dialogue, and problem solving

Intellectual Development

Language & Literacy

Language development is enhanced through:

  • Verbally communicating and listening to others
  • Dramatic play
  • Listening to and telling stories
  • Listening to music and singing songs
  • Beginning to identify meaningful words (own name, friends’ names, calendar words, job chart, etc.)
  • Dictating, drawing, and writing stories, and creating books of the child’s own making

Math

Math concepts are explored through play and hands-on activities, including:

  • Identifying shapes, sizes, patterns, and sequences
  • Sorting and categorizing into sets
  • Counting and comparison, including one-to-one correspondence
  • Measuring length and weight
  • Using numbers (as symbols and to represent quantity)
  • Graphing

Science

Science concepts are developed by:

  • Observing seasonal changes related to weather, plant life, animal behavior and activities of people/families
  • Sorting and categorizing items according to various properties
  • Observing and/or collecting plants, animals, and insects in their environment
  • Learning conservation of and respect for nature, including recycling materials and goods
  • Growing plants from seeds and bulbs, observing stages of growth and parts of plants
  • Exploring animals through hatching chicken eggs, butterflies, tadpoles, and more
  • Discovering how magnets work
  • Observing changes in matter through experiments with heat and cold
  • Cooking and eating healthy snacks

Social Studies

Social studies concepts are introduced by:

  • Learning about similarities and differences in peers and their families
  • Inviting family members to visit school and share history, traditions, culture, teach words in other languages, or read to the class
  • Exploring the community through field trips on the Washington University campus and surrounding neighborhood
  • Learning about occupations through books and personal visits and discussions
  • Experiencing multi-cultural customs with hands-on activities and cultural materials
  • Learning how people with disabilities experience life and practicing respect for each person as an individual
  • Role-playing about families, occupations, abilities, or traditions

Physical Development

Gross Motor Skills

Developed during indoor play and outdoors using:

  • Climbers, slides, trikes, scooters, swings
  • Balls to kick, throw and bounce; bean bags to toss; bubbles to chase
  • Sand to scoop, pour, dig
  • Simple games and sport skills such as throwing, hitting, and/or kicking a ball; playing hopscotch
  • Hiking and exploring the campus
  • Movement to music
  • Building with large and small wooden blocks

Fine Motor Skills

Developed throughout the day during activities such as:

  • Tracing, copying, writing letters of the alphabet, writing in journals, etc.
  • Using clipboards for portable writing or drawing in the classroom, on the playground, and during field trips
  • Drawing pictures, self-portraits, signing own name on paper, writing messages to friends
  • Working with manipulatives such as stringing beads, Legos, nuts and bolts, spinning tops
  • Sorting or creating a collage with small items like dried beans or peas

Visual Arts, Music & Drama

The emphasis in visual arts is on the process of exploring materials rather than the product. These experiences can build attention span, fine motor skills, confidence, emotional well-being, and independence.

Materials and experiences include:

  • Drawing with crayons, pencils, chalk, markers, sidewalk chalk
  • Painting with fingers, brushes, feathers, sponges, leaves, vegetables, and other materials
  • Printmaking with rubber stamps, potato shapes, and various other objects
  • Working with clay and doughs used for shaping, building, and modeling
  • Cutting, tearing, gluing, and stapling
  • Shaping/forming and stringing beads
  • Creating collages with fabrics, paper, pictures, and three-dimensional items
  • Building 3-dimensional constructions using boxes, paper tubes, cartons, and other recyclable materials
  • Creating pictures or structures using wire, clay, wood and other materials

Students also explore visual arts, music, and drama with specialists, in addition to everyday classroom and playground opportunities.

Assessment

Teachers observe students' activities throughout the year to learn about each child, plan for individual and group needs, and communicate with parents on an ongoing basis.

Assessment methods include:

  • Fall (informal): Teachers meet with parents, discuss activities and progress, and answer questions.
  • Spring (formal): Teachers complete an assessment covering all curriculum areas and send it home before the conference so families can review and prepare questions.
